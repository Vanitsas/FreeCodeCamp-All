Scientists Discover Ancient Forest Beneath Arctic Ice
Preserved trees offer a window into Earth's warmer past and clues about its future
By Dr. Amelia Hartwell, Science Correspondent

A team of researchers from the Nordic Institute of Geosciences announced a stunning find this week: a fully preserved ancient forest buried beneath nearly 300 meters of Arctic ice in northern Greenland. The trees, estimated to be over 2.8 million years old, appear to have been flash-frozen during a rapid glaciation event, leaving behind wood, leaves, and even intact root systems in remarkable condition.

Experts say the discovery could reshape our understanding of climate dynamics during the Pliocene epoch, when global temperatures were several degrees warmer than today. "This is like finding a time capsule," said lead researcher Dr. Søren Magnusson. "These trees tell us that Greenland was once a temperate, forested landscape — and studying them may help us model what parts of the Arctic could look like in the coming centuries as warming continues."
