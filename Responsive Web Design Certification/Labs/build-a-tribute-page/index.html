Ada Lovelace

Mathematician · Visionary · The World's First Programmer

· · ❦ · ·

A Life in Milestones

1815 Born in London to the poet Lord Byron and mathematician Anne Isabella Milbanke. Her mother, fearing she would inherit Byron's "madness," steered her firmly toward mathematics.
1833 At seventeen, introduced to Charles Babbage at a dinner party. She immediately grasped the potential of his unbuilt Difference Engine — a friendship and intellectual partnership began that would change history.
1835 Married William King, later Earl of Lovelace. She became Ada, Countess of Lovelace — but never ceased her mathematical pursuits, corresponding with the greatest scientific minds of the age.
1842–43 Translated an Italian engineer's memoir on Babbage's Analytical Engine from French. Her annotations — three times longer than the original — contained what is now recognised as the world's first computer algorithm.
1843 Published her landmark "Note G," describing how the engine could be programmed to compute Bernoulli numbers — a complete, step-by-step algorithm. She also foresaw that machines might one day compose music and manipulate symbols beyond mere numbers.
1852 Died at only thirty-six, from cancer — the same age her father Byron had died. Her work was largely forgotten for a century, until Alan Turing and the computing age rediscovered its profound prescience.
1980 The United States Department of Defense named a new programming language "Ada" in her honour — cementing her legacy as the patron saint of software.

"The Analytical Engine has no power of originating anything. It can only do what we know how to order it to perform."

— Ada Lovelace, 1843

A century before the digital age, Ada Lovelace saw not merely a calculating machine, but a universal engine of thought. Her vision of what computers could become — tools for creativity, music, and science — defines computing to this day.
